Armchair Psychology

By Addison Pozzi



God forgive me I might be a champagne socialist.

My mother used to tell me she’d shoot me in the foot

If I ever went into the military.

I used to say back, “Ma, I love that you think Obama made me patriotic”.

My predisposition to theatrics is making this difficult.


I want to tell you simply what’s happening.

I live on the border of two counties

Named God complex and involuntarily celibate.

And nothing is more dreadful to see

Then a note to myself in the morning.


You see, we live in a world of other people’s words.

I spent the summer writing about the rise of primitivism

But just stopped short of beard oil and ax-throwing bars.

Last week, I saw a man dragging around a bag

Of broken dancer’s feet.

Everything’s a sign when I don’t know what I’m looking for.


When mania climbs through my window,

I align with the colored illustration of a woman

Stomping bare bloody feet on broken glass

And declaring she is Leonardo Divinci.

My therapist calls these delusions of grandeur

Remedying medication and sleep

But, frankly, why would I kill the feeling

That I could kick God in the shins?


I’m making an argument.

When I die, give my blood plasma to the junkies,

Bury my cynicism with plastic flowers,

And let my headstone read,

“Here lies a white male self-aggrandizing intellectual

That tried to marginalize himself as much as possible.”

But don’t forget to send out thank-you notes to everyone

Who thought any better of me.


Last week, I bought a book called Make Time for Creativity.

I got high, read it in one sitting, and decided to get high again.

My laziness sometimes buys me gifts.

Addison Pozzi is a poet, essayist, and journalist currently residing in Nashville, TN. He has worked with This Wonderful World magazine for over two years, covering the Nashville music scene and his personal diatribes against it. You can find more of his work on social media under the handle @addisellsout on all platforms.
